# Maryland HB1389

## At a Glance

Maryland HB1389 is one of the more distinctive state reserve proposals because it does not start with a broad public-funds allocation cap. Instead, it builds a dedicated Bitcoin reserve fund around money obtained through the enforcement of certain gambling violations and then treats that fund as a state reserve asset.

## What This Would Actually Do

HB1389 would create the Maryland Bitcoin Reserve Fund and authorize the State Treasurer to treat Bitcoin as a reserve asset for the State through that fund. The bill’s core funding pathway would route money obtained by the State through enforcement of certain gambling violations into Bitcoin, while also allowing the reserve fund to receive money through budgetary and special-fund mechanisms.

## Bond / Reserve Analysis

**Supports:** direct reserve accumulation  
**Bond-financed reserve accumulation:** no meaningful support  
**Meaningful reading:** reserve-only, with a narrow special-fund funding path

Maryland matters because it shows a reserve concept built around a dedicated funding stream rather than a simple “let the Treasurer buy Bitcoin from any account” model. It is limited because the official record is still thin, the bill never produced a later committee result on the public page, and it does not include a financing mechanism.

## Key Statutory Mechanics

- Establishes the Maryland Bitcoin Reserve Fund as a state reserve-asset vehicle.
- Authorizes the State Treasurer to invest certain State-obtained funds in Bitcoin.
- Uses seized-gambling-proceeds statutes as a core source of money flowing into the fund.
- Treats Bitcoin as the reserve asset rather than creating a broader digital-asset basket.
- Embeds the concept in a special-fund structure rather than a generalized treasury-allocation cap.

## Why This Matters

Maryland is useful because it expands the registry beyond generic treasury-investment bills and shows how a reserve proposal can be tied to a narrow statutory funding source. That makes it an interesting policy record even though it is not especially strong for the bond-financing thesis.
