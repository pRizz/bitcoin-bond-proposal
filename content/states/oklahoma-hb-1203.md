# Oklahoma HB1203

## At a Glance

Oklahoma HB1203 is a meaningful reserve-side exemplar because it combines reserve branding with explicit public-fund investment caps and custody restrictions. It remains useful to the bond thesis mainly as a contrast case: it shows how a state can move reserve authority forward without creating a financing mechanism.

## What This Would Actually Do

HB1203 would create the Strategic Bitcoin Reserve Act and permit the State Treasurer to invest up to five percent of the State General Fund, Revenue Stabilization Fund, and Constitutional Reserve Fund in bitcoin, large-cap digital assets, and approved stablecoins. It would also allow state retirement plans to purchase eligible digital-asset exposure through exchange-traded products, subject to the same five-percent ceiling.

## Bond / Reserve Analysis

**Supports:** direct reserve or reserve-adjacent accumulation  
**Bond-financed reserve accumulation:** no meaningful support  
**Meaningful reading:** reserve-heavy

HB1203 matters because it shows a state-level willingness to apply explicit percentage caps, custody standards, and fund-level rules to digital-asset exposure. It is limited for the bond thesis because it does not pair reserve growth with a bond-financing structure.

## Key Statutory Mechanics

- Creates the Strategic Bitcoin Reserve Act.
- Limits State Treasurer purchases to five percent of specified state funds.
- Extends limited digital-asset investment authority to state retirement plans.
- Requires use of qualified custodians or exchange-traded products rather than unconstrained direct holding.
- Includes definitions for bitcoin, digital assets, exchange-traded products, and qualified custodians.

## Why This Matters

Oklahoma is a useful reserve-side benchmark because it gives the registry a more specific public-finance example than a generic “study Bitcoin” bill. It also helps show the difference between reserve authorization and the bond-financed reserve accumulation model this project prefers.
